Everglades National Park
Everglades National Park, located in southern Florida, is a sprawling and unique wetland ecosystem that holds global significance. Spanning over 1.5 million acres, the park is renowned for its rich biodiversity, distinctive landscape, and importance as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, a Wetland of International Importance, and an International Biosphere Reserve.

A Glimpse into History
The Everglades' history is rooted in its role as a vital ecosystem for indigenous tribes like the Calusa, Miccosukee, and Seminole, who lived in harmony with the wetlands. In the 19th and early 20th centuries, efforts to drain the Everglades for farming and urban development caused severe ecological damage. Conservation awareness grew in the 1930s, thanks to activists like Marjory Stoneman Douglas. In 1947, Everglades National Park was established to protect this unique subtropical wilderness. Today, it stands as a testament to the ongoing efforts to restore and preserve a globally significant ecosystem.
